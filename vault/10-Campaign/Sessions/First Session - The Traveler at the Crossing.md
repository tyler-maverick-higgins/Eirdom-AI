# First Session — The Traveler at the Crossing

## Summary

The campaign opens at the crossing-place on the edge of the wild, where a stranger arrives *wrong* — rimed in a frost that will not melt, a wrong cold pouring *out* of them from the deep East, and as Eirik and Pip work to hold it back, increasingly wrong in mind as the person recedes behind the cold. The traveler is slowly becoming a door for the thing rising in the East; the cold is what comes through first. They can be saved, but only by understanding what is happening to them — and the answer lies east, toward the source. This note is the GM's prep for that first session: the place, the stranger, how the wrongness reveals itself in stages, the slow decline that gives the mystery urgency, the findable seeds of a cure, and how Pip's ancient memory surfaces through what the players discover.

> The *spine* of this session was designed collaboratively and is settled (see the decision summary at the end). The *specific content* below — names, details, exact beats — is invented to give you a runnable first session, and is yours to change freely. Treat it as a strong first draft, not fixed canon. ## The Place — The Crossing

**[decide: this place needs a name.]** The last settled holding before the forest closes in, built where an old road crosses from North into East — a bridge-town, a ford with an inn. Northern traders and eastern wood-folk meet here under a truce older than anyone living. It belongs fully to neither world. That is why the story starts here.

What it should feel like in the first scene: ordinary, lived-in, a little tense at the edges (the truce is kept but not warm). Woodsmoke and mud. People who know each other. The forest is *right there* past the last building, and everyone keeps their back to it. This ordinariness matters — it is the grounded baseline the wrongness will violate.

## The Stranger

A traveler arrives at the crossing — **a stranger** to everyone here, which is part of the unease (no one to vouch for them, no one to grieve them, only the wrongness itself). They come from the East, from the forest side, on foot, in a bad way.

**Stage one — wrong in the body (the loud first beat, Eirik's domain):**
What the table *sees* first. The traveler is rimed in a frost that will not melt — but it is not *on* them, it is *coming out* of them. The cold spreads from somewhere inside, from the deep East they walked out of, and where it spreads the flesh goes grey-white and cold-burned and still, and the *person recedes* behind it. This is not frostbite. Something is opening a way *through* them, and the cold is what comes first — the way cold pours through a door cracked open onto winter. It is immediate, visible, and wrong: the rime-pattern creeping across skin, the unnatural chill in the air around them, the warmth leaving a living body. Eirik responds because steadying and protecting is what he does — and crucially, the cold is not contagious-on-touch, so he *can* approach, restrain, carry, and shield them. The horror is approachable, which is what lets the scene happen.

> [!note] Why cold, and why "through" (the design choice, delete once internalized)
> This is the settled affliction. Two things make it work: (1) The cold is the *medium* but the **door is the mechanism** — the body is a threshold the wrong cold is opening *through*, not just a curse afflicting it. This keeps it tied to the campaign's engine (becoming a door for the thing in the East) rather than being a generic ice-curse. (2) The cold theme is deliberate table-flavor (the players love the cold; the land is cold). Keep it as *one* wrongness — the cold-as-door — not cold plus other symptoms. One image the table can read instantly; two would muddy the first impression.

**Stage two — wrong in the mind (revealed as they help, Pip's domain):**
Once the traveler is being tended, the deeper wrongness surfaces — *not all at once*. As they work to warm them, to hold the cold back, the traveler speaks: words that aren't quite theirs, spoken in a voice gone flat and far-away, as if from the other side of the cold. A name. A direction — east. Fragments that don't cohere yet, surfacing in the gaps where the warmth almost reaches them and then losing ground again as the cold returns. They are alive but *receding*, and the more the cold advances, the less of the person answers. This is the thread that unspools over the session and beyond. Pip is the one who can *read* what is speaking through the cold — she recognizes the shape of it.

## The Clock — A Slow Decline Toward the Door

The traveler is **getting worse**, slowly, on a clock the GM holds (the players feel it; they never see the exact deadline). The failure state, known to the GM only: if they fail or run out of time, the traveler becomes a **door** — the thing rising in the East comes through, here, at the crossing.

> [!warning] Run the decline as a direction of travel, not a ticking bomb
> The dread comes from *slow* — every revelation costs something, the wrongness advances as they learn, and saving the person and solving the mystery are in tension. This keeps the slow burn from stalling. But it is winnable: do not play it as doom. The decline is a race they *can* win, which makes it tense rather than hopeless.

## Winnable — Plant the Cure in Scene One

**This is the part that takes real GM care.** Because the traveler *can* be saved, there must be a real, findable answer — and its seeds must be present from the start, even if the players don't recognize them yet. The cure is tied to **understanding the thing in the East**: to save the traveler, they must learn what is being done to them, and that knowledge lies toward the source.

Plant in this session, quietly:

- Something on or about the traveler that points east (where they came from, what they carry, what they say) — the first thread of the road.
- Something Pip half-recognizes (see below) — the first thread of the *answer*.
- A reason the cure is not here at the crossing — so the road east is necessary, not optional. **[decide: why can't it be solved locally? Because the source must be reached? Because only the thing's nature reveals the undoing?]**

The win condition does not need to be *solved* this session — it needs to be *seeded*, so that "we can save them, but we have to go east" is the door the session ends on.

## Pip's Memory — Triggered by Discovery, Not by Clock

Pip recognizes what is happening to the traveler — but **faintly at first**, and it lands harder as they investigate. Critically: **each resurfacing of her memory is triggered by something the players discover**, never by the GM deciding it's time.

- The rime-pattern spreading across the traveler's skin → Pip has *seen* cold move like that before, in a shape that isn't natural.
- The word the traveler repeats from the other side of the cold → Pip *knows* that word, from a long time ago.
- The *quality* of the cold itself — a wrong, reaching cold, not winter's honest cold → matches something in her four-hundred-year-old dread. (A forest-ancient of a cold land knowing the difference between true cold and *wrong* cold is character-perfect.)

Each discovery unlocks a piece of what Pip knows; each piece reframes the investigation. This keeps **both players active** — Eirik finds the things, Pip reads them — and keeps Pip central without turning Eirik into a bystander to her exposition. By session's end, faint recognition has become certainty, and it hits like a ton of bricks: she has seen this before, she did not like it then, and stopping it *here* does not mean it is stopped.

## The Tension You'll Have to Run — Carry or Leave

**[a real choice for you and Irina, flagged so it lands as a choice, not a plot hole.]** The cure lies east, but the traveler is declining. So: do they bring the failing traveler *with* them into the wild (fragile, slowing them, a constant ticking presence at their side), or leave them at the crossing (safer to move, but a clock now runs on someone they can't watch)? Either is valid and each makes a different campaign. Let the players choose; don't decide it for them. This tension — every mile east costs the person they're trying to save — is the engine's beating heart.

## How the Session Ends

On a **road east**. They have stabilized the immediate crisis but not solved it; the traveler can be saved but the answer is in the forest; Pip's recognition has turned the affliction from "one wrong stranger" into "the first sign of something old and rising." The session closes with the wild ahead of them and a reason to walk into it. The campaign opens *outward*.

## The Decision Summary (settled spine)

*The framework below was designed collaboratively and is canon. Build on it.*

- **Place:** crossing on the seam, edge of the wild (name [decide]).
- **Protagonists:** Eirik (North) and Pip (East), meeting over the event.
- **Event:** a stranger, arriving wrong — **body first, mind in layers.**
- **Engine:** the traveler is slowly becoming a **door** for the thing rising in the East — a real failure state on a GM-held clock.
- **Mode:** **winnable** — saveable, but only by understanding the source.
- **Direction:** the answer is **east**; the campaign opens outward.
- **Pip:** faint recognition → certainty, **triggered by player discovery**, not a timer.
- **Shape:** slow burn with a direction of travel, widening toward the rising power over a long campaign.

## Still Open (decide, or let emerge)

- Why the cure can't be found locally.
- What the rising power *is* (leave open — let play earn it).
- What broke Eirik's order, and whether it connects.
