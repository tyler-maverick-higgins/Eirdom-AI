# The Seam

## Summary

The Seam is the border country where the ordered North meets the old Eastern forest — not a line on a map but a band of contested, in-between land where the two worlds touch without blending. Here the last northern holdings thin out, the trees of the Ashwood begin to crowd the road, and folk of both worlds meet, trade, and distrust one another under truces older than memory. It belongs fully to neither the Holdlands nor the Ashwood; it is the friction between them made into a place. [[Whisperbridge]] sits at its heart, the chief crossing where the old road fords from North into East. The Seam is where the campaign begins and where its protagonists live — Eirik come from the North side, Pip from the wood side, both belonging a little to the Seam and fully to neither world. It is also where the wrongness rising in the East will first reach the world of men.

## Geography

A band of transitional country, neither open Holdland nor deep forest. Cleared ground gives way to scattered woods, then to the true treeline of the Ashwood; the land is wetter and rockier than the settled North, cut by cold rivers running down out of the forest. Old roads cross it — trade-roads worn by generations, fording rivers at shallow places like Whisperbridge. Travel is slow and weather-dependent; the rivers swell, the tracks turn to mud, and in hard winters the Seam can be cut off from the North entirely. The defining feature is the Ashwood's edge itself: a treeline everyone lives in sight of and no one turns their back to. Where exactly the forest "begins" is not fixed — folk on the Seam will tell you the edge moves, and lately, they say, it has been moving closer.

## History

Avoid speculation; rumor belongs elsewhere. What is established: the Seam has been a meeting-ground between North and East for longer than the present holdings have stood. The truces that govern its crossings — Whisperbridge's chief among them — predate anyone living and are honored out of mutual need rather than goodwill. Both worlds want the roads open. Beyond that, the Seam's deep history is largely unwritten and open to emerge in play.
